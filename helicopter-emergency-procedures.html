Helicopter Emergency Procedures — Autorotations and Engine Failures Explained

By HelipadUSA Staff · Updated June 2025 · 8 min read

One of the most common questions non-pilots ask about helicopters is: what happens if the engine stops? The assumption is that the helicopter simply falls out of the sky. The reality is very different. Helicopters are designed with a specific emergency procedure called an autorotation that allows a skilled pilot to land safely with a complete engine failure — no power required. This article explains exactly how it works, what other emergencies helicopter pilots train for, and why modern helicopters are safer than most people assume.

What Is an Autorotation?

An autorotation is a flight maneuver in which the helicopter descends through the air with the engine disengaged from the rotor system. As the helicopter descends, the upward flow of air through the rotor blades keeps them spinning — similar to the way a maple seed spins as it falls. That spinning rotor stores energy. At the right moment just above the ground, the pilot pulls up on the collective control to convert that stored rotor energy into lift, slowing the descent to a survivable landing speed.

Every helicopter is equipped with a freewheeling unit — essentially a one-way clutch — that automatically disconnects the engine from the rotor system if the engine fails or slows below rotor speed. This happens instantly and allows the rotor to continue spinning freely via autorotation rather than being dragged to a stop by a seized engine.

💡 The Key Fact Most People Don't Know A helicopter in autorotation is not falling — it is gliding. The descent rate is controlled, the rotor is spinning normally, and the pilot has full control of direction and landing site selection throughout the maneuver.

How Does a Pilot Execute an Autorotation?

The sequence happens fast but is thoroughly drilled into every helicopter pilot during training. Here is what occurs from engine failure to touchdown:

  1. Engine failure recognized — The low rotor RPM warning horn sounds. The pilot immediately lowers the collective to reduce blade pitch and preserve rotor RPM.
  2. Establish autorotation airspeed — The pilot adjusts cyclic to achieve the best autorotation airspeed for the aircraft, typically 60–80 knots for most light helicopters.
  3. Select a landing area — The pilot identifies the best available landing spot within gliding range. Open fields, roads, and parking lots are all viable options.
  4. Configure for landing — As the helicopter approaches the ground, the pilot adjusts the approach angle and slows forward speed.
  5. Flare — At approximately 40–100 feet above ground, the pilot pulls back on the cyclic to flare the helicopter. This dramatically slows forward speed and temporarily increases rotor RPM by changing the angle of airflow through the blades.
  6. Collective pull and touchdown — Just before touchdown, the pilot pulls up on the collective to convert the stored rotor energy into lift, cushioning the landing. A well-executed autorotation results in a firm but survivable touchdown.

How Much Altitude Do You Need for an Autorotation?

This is where it gets interesting. Most light helicopters can execute a successful autorotation from altitudes as low as 500 feet with sufficient forward airspeed. The critical danger zone for helicopters is called the Height-Velocity diagram — commonly known as the "dead man's curve." This chart shows combinations of altitude and airspeed where a successful autorotation may not be possible.

The most dangerous region is low altitude combined with low airspeed — hovering at 50 feet, for example. This is why helicopter pilots at low altitude always try to maintain some forward airspeed, which dramatically increases survivable autorotation options.

Other Emergency Procedures Helicopter Pilots Train For

Tail Rotor Failure

The tail rotor counteracts the torque of the main rotor and provides directional control. A tail rotor failure is one of the more serious helicopter emergencies because it can cause uncontrolled spinning. Pilots are trained to immediately increase airspeed — forward flight reduces the yawing tendency — and execute a running landing or autorotation depending on conditions. Most tail rotor failures are survivable with proper technique.

Hydraulic Failure

Most turbine helicopters use hydraulic systems to reduce the control forces required to fly the aircraft. A hydraulic failure makes the controls significantly heavier and less responsive. Pilots train to fly without hydraulics regularly so they can handle this emergency without panicking. Most hydraulic failures result in an uneventful precautionary landing at the nearest suitable area.

Engine Fire

An engine fire in a helicopter requires immediate action — the pilot shuts down the affected engine, activates the fire suppression system if equipped, and executes an emergency landing. Twin-engine helicopters can continue flying on one engine, giving the crew more time and options.

Settling With Power (Vortex Ring State)

This is a hazardous aerodynamic condition that can occur when a helicopter descends too steeply at low airspeed with power applied. The helicopter sinks into its own rotor downwash, causing a loss of lift that can compound rapidly. Recovery requires lowering the nose to gain airspeed and fly out of the vortex ring state. Pilots train specifically to recognize and avoid this condition.

Are Helicopters Safe?

Helicopter aviation has a higher accident rate than commercial airline travel — but that comparison is not particularly meaningful since they serve completely different operational environments. A more relevant comparison is general aviation, where helicopters and small airplanes operate in similar conditions. By that measure, modern helicopters flown by trained pilots are reasonably safe aircraft.

The majority of helicopter accidents involve pilot error, not mechanical failure. Continued flight into instrument meteorological conditions (flying into clouds without proper IFR training), low-level maneuvering, and fuel exhaustion account for a disproportionate share of accidents — all of which are preventable with proper training and discipline.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do helicopter pilots practice autorotations regularly? Yes. Autorotations are a standard part of recurrent training for all helicopter pilots. During practice, the instructor reduces power to idle to simulate an engine failure and the student executes the full maneuver down to a low hover or touchdown. Most pilots practice autorotations every few months.
Can a helicopter autorotate if both engines fail on a twin? Yes. Twin-engine helicopters can autorotate just like single-engine helicopters if both engines fail simultaneously. The freewheeling unit disconnects both engines from the rotor system and the pilot executes the same basic autorotation procedure.
How long does an autorotation take from start to landing? From engine failure to touchdown, a typical autorotation from 1,000 feet takes approximately 30 to 60 seconds depending on airspeed and descent rate. This gives the pilot meaningful time to select a landing area and configure for the approach — though it feels very fast in practice.
Is a helicopter autorotation landing rough? A well-executed autorotation in a training environment typically results in a firm touchdown similar to a normal landing. Emergency autorotations onto unprepared surfaces are rougher but survivable. The goal is not a perfect landing — it is a survivable one.
